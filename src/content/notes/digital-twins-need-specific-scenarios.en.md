I first saw an idea that human-in-the-loop can mean more than clicking confirm: a person’s continuing learning can be fed back into an Agent workflow. People do not “catastrophically forget” in the way a single session does, so long-term memory may genuinely matter.

Then, from February to March, I repeatedly came across very similar projects:

- Company A built a digital twin around personal experience and expression.
- Company B combined a twin with social matching; its public founding-team profile was full of top university backgrounds.
- Company C also used Agents to match and communicate first, with a top-PhD founder and a use case closer to dating.
- A major content platform was also hiring interns for twin-and-matching work.

The logic is understandable. If an Agent remembers someone’s experience, preferences, and needs, it can filter information, match people, and even handle a first conversation. Social networking, recruitment, and dating all have more information than people can process manually.

Still, a generic “build a version of me” feels a bit self-indulgent. I cannot even be bothered to tune the profiles of fictional characters I like, let alone maintain a personal Skill for years. A strong founding team does not prove ordinary users will train a twin.

Job search seems more plausible. CVs, preferences, and experience are relatively structured, so an Agent could filter roles and match requirements before a person makes the final call. The product should start from a specific task, not create a character first and search for something it can do.

The hard questions remain: memory cost, privacy boundaries, the safe level of automated communication, user confirmation, and whether cheaper models stay reliable over long context. A twin is only an interface concept. What matters is the task it actually completes.
