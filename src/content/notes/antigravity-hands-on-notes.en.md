I spent time properly trying Google Antigravity. At the time it was almost free, Gemini 3 Pro and Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking were available, and the quota was generous.

## Turning repeated steps into workflows

It could package earlier steps as a reusable workflow. Weekly reports, content planning, campaign reviews, and customer-research templates could all be fixed into a process first.

## Browser automation and action records

A browser subagent could operate Chrome—clicking, scrolling, and typing—and retain an action record for human review instead of merely claiming the work was done.

## Splitting large tasks

It planned first, worked in stages, and produced checkable artefacts such as tables, summaries, links, and screenshots. Multiple Agents could also work in parallel.

My impression was that this was useful beyond development, especially for operations, product, and sales work involving browsers and repeated processes.

I later returned to Codex more often because its interface, project boundaries, and Diff experience fit me better. But Antigravity showed me early that AI coding tools were becoming work environments made of workflows, browsers, artefacts, and multiple Agents—not merely chat that writes code.
