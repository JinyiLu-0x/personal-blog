For study tasks where AI use is clearly allowed, I combine Codex, voice input, and Obsidian:

1. Only allow access to local folders relevant to the current topic.
2. Extract concepts, terminology, and questions that still need checking.
3. Organise the material into Markdown, checklists, or Mermaid diagrams.
4. Compare it against the original materials and add course context and my own understanding.
5. Decide myself what should be kept, changed, or deleted.

This reduces time spent copying, formatting, and repeatedly sorting material, so more attention can go into understanding and verification. AI can plan, break work down, and call tools, but people still define the boundaries, provide missing material, catch errors, and take responsibility for the result.

With Codex + Typeless + Obsidian, ordinary coursework can be partly automated when AI is not restricted. Human input is usually where the work needs a final touch and a careful check.

> We used to call AI a copilot for humans. Increasingly, humans can look more like subagents for AI: AI plans, decomposes, and schedules; people click confirm, provide missing information, send the result, and carry the consequences.

This workflow only applies where course policy allows it. It is not used to generate assessment answers where AI is prohibited, and it does not replace necessary personal practice.
