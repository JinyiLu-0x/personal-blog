I first saw a simple argument: software suits B2B because selling one copy or a thousand takes similar production effort, while physical products add logistics, storage, and inventory every time.

That initially felt convincing. I even used it to explain how the more B2B- and developer-oriented Claude could catch up with the far more widely recognised ChatGPT, especially after Claude Code. Public recognition and the ability to earn money from users are not the same thing.

Then I realised AI is not traditional SaaS. Copying software is cheap, but every AI call consumes compute. More users can spread fixed costs while also multiplying inference costs. “One copy and a thousand copies cost the same” cannot be applied directly.

User habit is another factor. An older business owner told me he was seeing more people around him use one mass-market AI product and believed they formed a paying market. I initially disagreed because many were free users and the model did not seem indispensable. His point was that habits are difficult to change, like people staying with the same social app. That correction made sense: model quality is not the only variable; habit can create retention.

Further corrections:

- competition in B2C does not remove the opportunity to improve an established product pattern;
- emotion, brand, and habit can all create consumer retention;
- enterprise customers are not automatically loyal and will keep negotiating price;
- B2B may require convincing fewer people, but sales cycles, integration cost, and organisational risk are higher;
- simplicity often matters more than feature depth, but trust and go-to-market still matter.

I also had some imperfect but useful examples. DeepSeek’s cheap tokens may be subsidised by a different business logic. YouMind looks like a knowledge tool for writers, but I am still unsure whether it is really B2B or B2C. Recommending it to a business owner does not automatically make it an enterprise purchase.

This connects to my earlier growth note: even within B2C, differences in recommendation, retention, and monetisation create very different affordable acquisition costs. [Growth and monetisation are really the same unit economics](/en/notes/growth-and-monetization).

So I no longer say “software cannot do B2C” or “B2B is always better.” They have different cost structures, buying decisions, and retention systems.
