I knew about Obsidian in 2024, but honestly it feels like an unfinished apartment: you need to configure plugins yourself, which is annoying at first. An attachment-management plugin feels essential once images accumulate, and cloud sync is another choice. I keep mobile lighter and use more plugins on Mac.

I used OneNote more before. As my notes became more concise and I used AI more often to organise things, I moved towards Obsidian. Copying AI output into Markdown renders cleanly, and PDF export is less likely to break than in traditional note software.

The disadvantage is configuration; the advantage is flexibility. Editing beside preview feels slightly geeky and the graph needs training, but I still like it.

Most plugins are open source. Attachments, contents, sync, and graphs can be added gradually around personal habits rather than installed as one heavy system.

In study contexts where AI is allowed, I let tools read a specified local folder, extract course material, and organise it into Markdown, checklists, or Mermaid diagrams. The human still defines scope, checks sources, and adds the important points.

Some people connect Obsidian directly to Claude Code, Cursor, or Copilot-style tools. With a long history of notes, an Agent may gradually understand the material, but I prefer to treat it as retrieval and organisation over local files—not “the thing that knows me best.”

On Mac I would also recommend Arc for organising pages and immersive browsing. I used it for a long time and fall back to Chrome when Arc is buggy.
