Technical success is not product success, and a successful product does not always require particularly deep technology.

Experienced engineers can quickly see through the “technical essence” of a product and decide it is nothing special. But many great products never needed extreme technical sophistication.

SSH + Claude Code may already solve most technical problems. From the product side, the question is not whether an expert can assemble it, but whether ordinary users know how to install it, grant permissions, keep using it, and what task it actually solves.

OpenClaw can create scheduled tasks in natural language. That does not mean an autonomous intelligence has appeared. But turning complex capabilities into something ordinary people can use is still product work.

So I now separate two questions:

1. Is there a genuinely new technical capability?
2. Has an existing capability been placed inside a use case that actually works?

Answering only one is not enough to judge a product’s value.
