Most tools in this list are things I have not actually tried. I came across them by accident, marked them, and want to test them one by one later.

## AI video

🐎 **Remotion + HyperFrames**

This was an AI video-production direction I saw. Most AI currently generates content, but there still is not much that directly performs “CapCut-style editing operations” as an Agent.

My thought was whether APIs could connect the workflow: if A1 is weak on its own, can A2 assist it, and can another model operate an embedded browser? Rebuilding software is faster in the AI era. A reusable personal video workflow might combine Remotion and HyperFrames, restrict the AI to fixed Skills, and force it to follow the workflow.

## 📈 Quant and finance

🌟 **Quant Agent**

I do not mean “ask AI which stock will rise.” I mean the opposite: do not let it make subjective guesses; let it run data, backtest, and write code without handing it the final decision.

I currently think of a quantitative Agent as a toolchain:

- acquire and clean market, financial-statement, news, or other structured data;
- turn an idea into a testable rule instead of relying on feeling;
- run it through historical data and inspect returns, drawdown, win rate, and turnover;
- generate Python, SQL, backtesting scripts, or visualisations;
- warn about overfitting, small samples, transaction costs, slippage, and T+1 limits;
- translate backtest output into charts and conclusions people can read.

My boundary is that AI can handle data and engineering, but it should not make the final call. Retail investors in China cannot do high-frequency trading in the same way institutions can; “quant” cannot merely sound advanced without respecting real constraints.

This is still an empty slot for future exploration. I have not run a real backtest or built a project, so I am not turning it into an investment conclusion.

## Job search and interviews

I still need to practise LeetCode, mainly for large-company interviews. More aggressive teams may look directly at how someone uses AI—even how much quota they are willing to subscribe to—because a plan above US$100 a month at least suggests genuinely frequent use.

But hiring processes change at different speeds. I still do not know whether Australian interviews expect live coding in the same way or what they specifically assess; my impression is that change may be slower than at large Chinese technology companies. Web3 hiring is also weaker than before, with fewer startup investors and therefore fewer roles.

For now these are all things to try, not usage conclusions. The next step is to run one small task end to end and record it separately.
